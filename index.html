Introduction

The cultivation of paddy (rice) and gram (chickpea/pulse) represents a fundamental dichotomy in global agriculture, spanning the globe's most water-rich and most semi-arid farming systems. Both crops are essential for human nutrition, yet their cultivation requirements are vastly different.

Importance of Irrigation

  • Maintains soil moisture for better germination.
  • Supports plant growth during dry periods.
  • Improves flowering and pod filling stages.
  • Prevents flower drop due to water stress.

Step-by-Step Irrigation Process

  1. Pre-sowing irrigation:Seedling Preparation: Select, treat, and sow seeds in a small, raised nursery bed to grow healthy seedlings (for about 20-30 days).
  2. Seedling stage:Puddling and Levelling: The main field is flooded and then churned (puddled) to soften the soil, control weeds, and create a water-impervious layer before being perfectly levelled.
  3. Vegetative stage:Transplanting: Young seedlings are carefully removed from the nursery and replanted at a uniform spacing into the prepared, puddled main field.
  4. Flowering stage:Water Management: The field is maintained with a continuous shallow layer of water (flooding) or managed using water-saving techniques like Alternate Wetting and Drying (AWD).
  5. Pod filling stage:Nutrient Management: Fertilizers (NPK) are applied at specific growth stages (e.g., tillering and panicle initiation) to ensure robust crop development.
  6. Before harvest:Weed and Pest Control: Weeds are managed manually or chemically, and the crop is monitored for common pests and diseases throughout the growing season.
  7. Harvest and Post-Harvest: The water is drained; the mature, golden-yellow crop is cut (Harvesting), grains are separated from the stalks (Threshing), and finally, the grain is dried for safe storage.

Irrigation Methods

Common irrigation methods suitable for red gram include:

  • Continuous Submergence (Flooding):Method: Keeping the rice field continuously covered with a shallow layer of standing water (typically 5-10 cm) throughout most of the growing season.
  • Alternate Wetting and Drying (AWD):Method: The field is flooded, then allowed to dry until the water level drops well below the soil surface (monitored with a tube), and then re-flooded.
  • System of Rice Intensification (SRI):Method: Focuses on maintaining the soil in a moist but non-saturated condition, especially during the early stages, rather than continuous flooding.
  • Drip Irrigation (Modern/Precision):Method: Delivers small, precise drops of water directly to the plant's root zone via a network of tubes/emitters.

Conclusion

Paddy (rice) cultivation is foundational to global food security, particularly in Asia, but its traditional methods are ecologically and economically unsustainable in the face of climate change, water scarcity, and rising input costs.
